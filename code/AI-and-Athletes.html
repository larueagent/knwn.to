That's not a you problem. That's not even an AI problem. That's a context problem.

Every Conversation Starts From Zero

AI doesn't remember you.

Not what you told it last week. Not your sport, your position, your team, your history, your coaching relationship, your injury record, your mental game, your goals, your fears, or the thing that happened in that semifinal two years ago that you still think about. Every time you open a session, it knows nothing about you — until you tell it.

Most athletes never tell it.

They type a question. They get an answer. The answer is fine. They move on. They come back tomorrow, type another question, and AI has forgotten everything again. The same blank slate, every time.

This is the core problem with how almost every athlete uses AI right now. They treat it like a search engine — ask a question, get a result. And a search engine that talks back is genuinely useful. But it is a fraction of what's possible when AI actually knows you.

Two Athletes, Same Question

Athlete A types: "How do I stay focused in tight moments?"

AI answers with the standard toolkit: one cue at a time, controlled breathing, pre-performance routine, trust your preparation. Fine. Nothing wrong with it. Nothing personal about it either.

Athlete B opens AI with a file she built — her athlete.md. It tells AI everything: she's a D1 midfielder, junior year, playing through a coaching change that disrupted the offensive system she was recruited for. Her best games happen when she feels physically loose and socially connected to her teammates. Her worst happen when she's in her head about whether she's doing enough to deserve her starting spot. She has a habit of over-controlling the ball in tight moments, trying to manufacture something instead of reading and reacting.

What's a .md file? The .md stands for markdown — a plain text format with minimal formatting. No special software, no app required. You can open it in any text editor, paste it directly into any AI, and edit it like a document. The reason we use it: AI reads it cleanly. No noise, just the words you wrote.

She types the same question: "How do I stay focused in tight moments?"

AI answers: "Given what I know about your game — you tend to tighten up around the question of whether you're doing enough. In tight moments, that usually shows up as you trying to create instead of read, over-touching the ball when the simpler play is right there. The focus question isn't really about staying present — it's about trusting your read. One thing worth trying: before you receive the ball, make your decision early. What does the space tell you before the ball arrives? That pre-decision habit takes the 'am I doing enough' question off the table. You've already answered it."

Same AI. Same technology. Completely different conversation — because one athlete gave it context and one didn't.

Why This Matters More for Athletes

Athletes live in a world of feedback that is simultaneously constant and incomplete.

Your coach watches film. Your trainer tracks your load. Your parents watch from the stands. Everyone sees a piece of you — and almost nobody sees the full picture. The performance data lives in one app. The mental data lives nowhere except your own head. The injury history lives in a training room somewhere. None of it connects.

What would actually help is something that holds all of it together. Something that knows your physical history and your mental patterns and your coaching relationship and your goals and sees them as one picture.

That's what athlete.md does. It's a file that holds the full picture of you as a competitor. Built once, updated over time, readable by any AI you use.

"But ChatGPT already remembers me." True — and it's useful. But AI memory is the AI holding your information, in its own format, in its own system. You can't read it, you can't hand it to a coach or a sports psychologist, and when you switch tools it's gone. Your athlete.md is you holding your information. One is a convenience feature. The other is infrastructure you own, can inspect, can share, and can carry forward regardless of which AI you use next year.

The blank slate problem, solved.

What a Portrait Is — and Isn't

A First Read portrait is not a psychological assessment. It's not a type or a category or a score. It doesn't put you in a box.

It's a mirror. The words are yours — organized, reflected back, made usable.

LaRue's job in First Read isn't to evaluate you. It's to help you see yourself clearly enough that AI — any AI, not just LaRue — can actually help. Every line in the portrait is there because it changes what useful looks like. Marcus's portrait tells AI: when he goes quiet, that's the signal. Dani's portrait tells AI: the sequence matters more than the feeling.

Load that portrait and the conversation that follows is different. Not because AI got smarter. Because it knows something true.

The Six Sections

Every First Read portrait has the same six sections:

The Portrait — Who you are and where your competitive identity came from. A narrative origin story, not a summary.

Competitive Identity — Your behavioral patterns under normal conditions. How you show up when things are going well.

Pressure Profile — What happens when it gets hard. Physically, mentally, the specific patterns that emerge under real stakes.

Relationships and Environment — Who helps, who doesn't, what's unsaid about your support system.

The Direction — What you actually want, reframed clearly. Not your stated goals — the real aim underneath them.

Metadata — The machine-readable layer. Your ACSI indicators, whether wearables are connected, which tier you're on. This is what the platform builds on. It's also what makes your portrait a living document rather than a one-time snapshot.

Who You Are as an Athlete

athlete.md Section 1: Identity

Before we build anything, one question.

Not your stats. Not your PR. Not your win-loss record or your ranking or what your coach said about you last week.

This one: What does being an athlete mean to you?

Not what you do. Who you are when you're doing it.

Most athletes have never been asked that directly. They've been asked about goals. About times and results and what they want to improve. But the question underneath all of that — the one that actually shapes how you compete — almost never gets asked out loud.

We're asking it now. Because it's the first thing your file needs.

Shortcut: Complete your First Read at knwn.to/first-read. LaRue will build this section for you in 15 minutes. Then come back and add anything it missed.

What Identity Tells AI

When you open a conversation with AI and ask for help, AI is making assumptions. It doesn't know you yet, so it fills the blank with a default: a generic athlete. Probably somewhere in the middle of the performance spectrum. It gives you advice that works for that person.

If that's not you, the advice is off.

The Identity section fixes the foundation. It tells AI:

  • What sport you play, and at what level
  • What position or event you compete in
  • How long you've been doing this
  • What it actually means to you — not the polished answer, the real one

That last one matters more than the rest combined.

Two athletes can play the same sport, same position, same level, and have completely different relationships to it. One plays because she loves competition and the game itself — losses sting but don't linger. The other has been doing this since he was six, and it's wrapped up in his identity, his family, his future. A loss doesn't just feel bad. It feels like information about who he is.

Same sport. Same level. Completely different mental landscape.

AI can't help you navigate that landscape if it doesn't know which one you're in.

The Question Most Athletes Avoid

What does being an athlete mean to your identity?

There's a version athletes answer easily: "I love competing. I love my team. Sports taught me discipline." That's the college essay version. Not wrong. Also not what we need.

The version we need sounds more like:

  • "I've been defined by this sport since I was nine. I don't know who I am without it."
  • "I play because I genuinely love it, but I'm terrified of what happens when it ends."
  • "My parents sacrificed a lot for this. I feel the weight of that every time I compete."
  • "This is the one place I feel like I actually belong."
  • "Honestly? I'm not sure I'd still be doing this if the scholarship wasn't part of it."

Any of those is a real answer. All of them tell AI something it needs to know — because how you relate to your sport shapes how you respond to pressure, what failure means, and what your best performance actually feels like.

You don't have to figure all of that out right now. You just have to start being honest about it.

A Note on Level

Be specific about where you are.

"Competitive" means nothing to AI. "D3 collegiate sophomore" means everything. So does "high school JV, first year on the team" or "club travel, U16, one of the top three players on my team."

The level tells AI what kind of pressure you're under, what the stakes feel like, what a realistic outcome looks like for someone at your stage. A freshman walk-on and a senior captain might ask the same question — but the right answer is completely different.

Don't undersell yourself. Don't oversell either. Just be accurate.

That entry tells AI: this athlete carries weight when they compete. Losing hits differently for them. Reassurance alone won't help — it'll need to go deeper than "you'll get them next time."

All of that from six sentences.

Chapter 4

How You Actually Perform

athlete.md Section 2: Performance Profile

Before we talk about patterns or what your coach says about your game — stop.

Put your hand on your chest.

What does it feel like right before your best performance? Not what you're thinking. What you're feeling. In your body. Your shoulders, your jaw, your breath. The specific physical sensation of being ready.

Most athletes can tell you what a great result looks like. Fewer can tell you what it feels like from the inside — the signals in the body that show up before the scoreboard does.

That's what this chapter is about. Because AI can only help you manage what you can describe. And your body is telling you things your stats never will.

Results vs. Readiness

Here's a distinction that changes how you use AI.

Results are what happened. Final score. Your time. Whether you won or lost. Results are out of your control — a lot goes into a result that has nothing to do with you.

Readiness is what you brought. Your energy level going in. How settled or scattered your mind was. Whether your body felt loose or locked. Whether you were present or already somewhere else. Readiness is yours. Entirely.

AI cannot help you control results. It can help you understand, track, and manage your readiness. That's the job. And to do that job, it needs to know what your readiness actually looks like — for you, in your body, on your best days and your worst.

What "Locked In" Actually Feels Like

Ask ten athletes what peak performance feels like and most will say the same things: "I was in the zone." "I wasn't thinking." "Everything just flowed."

Useful as a description. Not useful as data.

Here's the question that makes it useful: Where do you feel it?

For some athletes, locked in feels like physical looseness — like their body is warmed up and responsive, like they're a half-second ahead of the play. For others, it's mental quiet — the inner narrator goes silent and they're just moving. For others, it's connection — they feel tuned in to the people around them, picking up signals, making reads before anything develops.

Your version is specific to you. And AI needs the specific version — not "I was in the zone" but "I felt loose in my hips and my breathing was slow and I stopped thinking about what I was supposed to do."

That sentence tells AI something it can use.

Your Warning Signs

Every athlete has them. The signals that show up before performance drops — before they even know it's happening.

A volleyball player starts serving short. A swimmer's stroke rate creeps up. A basketball player stops calling plays and goes quiet. A pitcher's shoulders come up toward their ears.

These are warning signs. They're the body announcing that something is off — that pressure is arriving, that doubt is creeping in.

Most athletes don't notice their warning signs until after the fact: yeah, I was tight from the first possession. I knew something was off. I just couldn't stop it.

Naming your warning signs gives you a chance to catch them earlier. And when AI knows them, it can reflect them back to you — before a game, after a hard session, when you're debriefing a performance that didn't go the way you wanted.

"You mentioned you felt your shoulders coming up during warmup. That's one of your signals. What did you do with it?"

That question only comes from something that knows your patterns.

What Derails You

Different from warning signs. Warning signs are the signal. Derailment is the cause.

Some athletes derail when they feel like they have to prove something — a scout in the stands, a coach who doubted them. The external judgment shifts their internal state and suddenly they're performing for the wrong audience.

Some athletes derail when they fall behind early — not because they can't come back, but because they start pressing, trying to make up the deficit in one play.

Some athletes derail when they don't feel challenged in warmup. They need to feel pushed to feel ready, and when that doesn't happen, they coast into the game flat.

None of these are flaws. They're patterns. And patterns can be worked with — once you know what they are.

What You Do Well Under Pressure

This section earns its place.

A lot of performance work focuses on what goes wrong. This question flips it: what goes right, specifically, when things get hard?

Some athletes get more focused under pressure, not less. Some get quieter and more efficient. Some perform better when there's something at stake — they need the meaning to activate.

You probably know what yours is. That belongs in your file. Because AI can reinforce it — not to hype you up, but because it's true, and you need to hear it from something that knows your full picture.

Chapter 5

Your Mental Game — The Honest Version

athlete.md Section 3: Mental Performance Patterns

Every athlete has two games.

The public game — what the team sees, what the coach sees, what shows up on film. The composure. The confidence. The version you've learned to project because sport rewards the athlete who looks like they have it together.

And the private game. What's actually happening inside. The voice that shows up after a bad play. The doubt that spikes before a big moment. The replay loop that runs at 2am after a loss. The way confidence sometimes feels solid and sometimes feels like you borrowed it and aren't sure you get to keep it.

Most performance advice addresses the public game. This chapter is about the private one.

Not to expose it. Not to fix it. To describe it accurately enough that AI can actually help.

Why This Is the Hardest Section

You've been trained — probably for years — to project confidence. To keep your head up after a mistake. To look like you belong even when you're not sure you do. That training is useful. It's also the reason this section is hard to write.

What we need here isn't the projected version. It's the actual one.

If you describe your mental game the way you'd describe it to a coach who's evaluating you, you'll write something like: "I bounce back well from mistakes. I stay focused under pressure. I work hard."

That's fine. It's also not useful.

What's useful: "After a bad play, I go quiet for two or three possessions. I tell myself I'm fine but my decision-making gets cautious and I stop taking chances. It usually passes but I can't always stop it mid-game."

That's the version AI can work with. That's what makes the difference between generic advice and something that actually fits.

How You Handle Failure

This is different for every athlete, and most athletes have a specific pattern they can recognize once they stop performing for the room.

Some athletes externalize: the call was bad, the conditions were tough, the team let them down. Not always wrong — sometimes those things are true. But when externalizing is the default response to every setback, it blocks the learning that could make things different next time.

Some athletes internalize hard: one mistake becomes a referendum on whether they belong. A bad game leads to a bad week leads to wondering if this is even for them. The failure doesn't just sting — it sticks.

Some athletes move on fast — almost too fast. They've learned to protect themselves by not letting results land. Which works for mental protection but sometimes misses what the failure was trying to tell them.

Most athletes are a combination of these, depending on the kind of failure and the context it happens in.

Your pattern isn't a flaw. It's a pattern. Write what actually happens in the hour after a bad performance — not the version where you "stay positive and learn from it." What you actually do, feel, and think.

Your Inner Critic

Every athlete has one. The voice that runs commentary on the gap between where you are and where you think you should be.

Some inner critics are harsh and loud. Some are subtler — not a shout but a whisper: are you sure you're good enough for this?

What does yours say? Not in general — specifically. What's its line? What's the thing it comes back to?

For some athletes it's comparison: those other athletes are catching up, and you've stopped improving. For some it's timeline: you should be further along by now. For some it's belonging: everyone in this room earned their place, and you're not sure you did.

When AI knows what your inner critic says, it can interrupt the pattern instead of accidentally reinforcing it. "You mentioned your confidence leaks late in close games when you start thinking about outcome. You're in the fourth quarter. What's one next play?" That question only comes from something that knows your pattern.

Where Confidence Comes From — and Where It Leaks

Confidence isn't the same for every athlete.

Some athletes build it through preparation — if they've done the work, they can trust the work. Walk in undertrained and their confidence wobbles.

Some build it through performance — they need early success in a game or session to feel locked in. They can't generate it in a vacuum.

Some build it through connection — they need to feel the team, feel the energy. Isolation deflates them.

Your version is worth knowing. Because AI can remind you of it when it matters — before a game when you're doubting yourself, after a string of poor performances when you're trying to find your footing again.

And the flip side: where does it leak? What's the situation that reliably drains your confidence even when you've done everything right? Knowing where the leak is gives you a chance to patch it — or at least see it coming.

What This Looks Like in Your File

## Mental Performance Patterns

How I handle failure: I go quiet. I replay it. I'm fine by the next day but the hour after is rough — I pull inward and stop talking.

My inner critic: Tells me I peaked too early. That other people are catching up.

Where confidence comes from: Preparation. If I've done the work, I can trust the work.

Where it leaks: Late in close games when I start thinking about outcome instead of next play. When someone I respect seems disappointed in me.

Specific. Private. Honest. That's what makes it work.

Chapter 6

What You're Working Toward

athlete.md Section 4: Goals and Timeline

You probably have goals. Most athletes do.

What most athletes don't have is a clear answer to the question underneath the goal: why does this matter to you?

Not the answer you'd give a recruiter or a coach. The real one. The one that gets you out of bed on the days when nothing is working and you'd rather not.

That's what this chapter is about — and it's the part AI needs most.

The Plan Problem

AI is very good at making plans. Give it a goal and it will give you steps, timelines, checkpoints, progress markers. It will be thorough and logical and completely disconnected from what actually drives you.

Because plans aren't the problem. Most athletes know what they're supposed to do. The problem is the days when you don't want to do it — when the training is hard and the results aren't there and the goal feels abstract and far away.

On those days, a plan doesn't help. The why behind the plan does.

When AI knows why your goal matters — not just what it is — it can speak to the right thing. It can remind you of the reason when the reason is what you need.

"Make varsity" is a goal. AI can help you plan for it.

"Make varsity because my dad played here and I want him to see me in that jersey before he retires" is a goal AI can help you carry.

Those are different kinds of help. The second requires knowing the why.

Three Kinds of Goals

It helps to know which kind you're working with.

Outcome goals are results: make the team, drop time, win the championship, get the scholarship. They matter. They give you direction. But AI can't control outcomes — too many variables outside you. If your only goals are outcomes, AI can help you prepare, but it can't give you the result.

Process goals are behaviors: show up to every practice. Watch film once a week. Get eight hours of sleep during the season. These are entirely within your control, and AI is excellent at helping with them — tracking, reminding, adjusting when life gets in the way.

Identity goals are about who you're becoming: be the kind of teammate people want next to them in a hard moment. Leave this sport knowing you gave it everything, not wondering what would have happened if you had.

Identity goals are the most powerful and the least talked about. They don't show up in a box score. But they shape everything — how you train, how you treat your teammates, how you respond when things get hard.

Your file should have at least one of each. But the identity goal is the one that makes AI's help feel like it means something.

The Why Is Not Optional

Two athletes can have the exact same goal — make the starting lineup — and have completely different experiences working toward it, because the reasons are different.

One athlete wants to start because she's worked four years for it and starting represents the payoff of that investment. When she's struggling, what she needs is a reminder that the work is already done — trust it.

Another athlete wants to start because his confidence is tied to whether the coach believes in him. When he's struggling, what he needs is something different entirely — a reminder that his value isn't determined by a starting spot.

Same goal. Different reasons. Completely different help.

AI can only give you the right help if it knows which athlete you are. The why tells it.

About the Long Game

Include where you want this to go — not just this season.

Not because AI needs a five-year plan. But because knowing whether you're playing for the love of it, playing for a scholarship, playing while quietly wondering if this is the last year — that context changes everything.

An athlete who is chasing a scholarship needs different conversations than an athlete in their final season who just wants to leave having given everything.

Neither is better. Both are real. And AI adjusts when it knows which situation it's in.

Section 5: Communication and Coaching Style

Here's something most athletes have never been asked.

Not "what are your goals?" Not "what are you working on?" Not "how's the season going?"

This: If you could design exactly how the people in your corner talk to you — what would that look like?

Most athletes have never had the chance to answer that question. You adapt to whoever's coaching you. You learn their style, figure out what they respond to, adjust. You make it work.

This chapter flips it. For the first time, you get to describe the communication style that actually works for you — and AI will follow it exactly.

AI Has No Ego

This is one of the things that makes AI genuinely different from human coaches.

Your coach has a preferred style. Some coaches are direct to the point of blunt. Some build you up before they break things down. Some are emotional, some clinical, some use humor, some never do. That style isn't wrong — it's just theirs. And adapting to it is part of playing for them.

AI has no preferred style. None. It's not attached to being the kind of coach who "tells it like it is" or the kind who "meets athletes where they are." It has no ego invested in its own approach.

That means it will communicate exactly the way you tell it to. Direct or gentle. Brief or detailed. Challenging or supportive. Technical or emotional. It will be whatever you say works best for you — consistently, every time, without a bad day getting in the way.

But it can only do that if you tell it. Which is what this section is for.

How You Receive Feedback Best

Think about the best feedback you've ever gotten. Not the nicest — the most useful. The kind that actually changed something.

What made it land?

For some athletes, feedback lands when it's direct: no softening, no preamble, just what needs to change and why. They find the cushioned version condescending.

For others, direct feedback without context lands as criticism. They need to understand the why before they can hear the what.

Some athletes need the relationship established before feedback means anything. They need to feel like the person giving feedback actually knows them and is on their side.

Your version is specific to you. You've had the experience of feedback that landed and feedback that bounced — you can feel the difference.

Write the version that landed. Tell AI what that looks like.

Push or Pull

Some athletes need to be pushed. Challenge is the on-switch. If the bar isn't high enough, they coast. They need someone who asks the harder question, holds them to the standard, doesn't accept "good enough."

Some athletes need to be pulled. They're already hard on themselves. Adding pressure doesn't raise performance — it compresses it. What they need is someone who believes in them clearly, who gives them room to trust themselves.

A lot of athletes are somewhere in between — or the answer depends on the moment. A hard week of training might call for pull. A week when they've been coasting might call for push.

You can tell AI this. You can even tell it to read the situation: "if I seem like I'm being too easy on myself, push me; if I seem already hard on myself, back off." AI will use that instruction every time.

What Shuts You Down

Equally important: what doesn't work.

Some athletes shut down with public criticism. Some shut down when they feel managed instead of developed — when a coach goes easy on them, pulls punches. Some shut down when feedback comes immediately after a bad performance — they need space to process first.

None of these are weaknesses. They're just how you work. And knowing them means AI won't accidentally trip them.

A Note for Parents

If you're a parent reading this chapter alongside your athlete, you might recognize some of what's here.

The way your athlete describes feedback that shuts them down — or the difference between being pushed and being pulled — is probably true in your relationship too, not just with coaches. Most athletes haven't had the words to say it directly. This chapter gave them some.

You don't have to respond to it right now. But if something in these pages sounds familiar, it might be worth a quiet conversation. That conversation tends to go better when the athlete gets to answer first.

Chapter 8

Your History and Right Now

athlete.md
Sections 6 + 7: Background and Current State

This chapter covers two sections at once — because they're opposites, and understanding both together makes each one clearer.

Background is the slow-moving part of your file. The injuries, the transitions, the formative moments. What a new coach would want to know before working with you. It changes rarely — once a season if something significant happens, otherwise it just grows.

Current State is the fast-moving part. The one you update. Before competitions. After hard losses. Any time something shifts. This is the section that keeps your portrait connected to your actual life instead of a version of you from three months ago.

Background is the foundation. Current State is the pulse.

Part One: Background

What AI Can't See Without History

When you open a session with AI and describe what you're dealing with — pressure before a big game, a slump that won't break, doubt about whether you belong — AI responds to what you've described.

But a lot of what drives those feelings lives in the past, not the present.

The athlete who hesitates on cuts might not realize their body still remembers a knee injury from two years ago. The athlete who spirals after a poor performance might not connect it to a season three years back when a string of bad games cost them their starting spot.

None of that is visible to AI if you don't put it in the file.

When you do, something shifts. AI stops responding to just what's happening now and starts seeing patterns across time. It can ask the question that connects the present to the history — and sometimes that question is the one that finally cracks something open.

This Is Not Therapy

You are not being asked to process your history. You're not being asked to work through anything or make peace with the past.

You're just naming it.

"I had a serious knee injury junior year. Fourteen-month recovery. I came back faster but I play safer now than I used to, and I'm not sure that's right." That's enough. AI will factor it in. It doesn't need the whole story — just enough to know the pattern exists.

The only question worth asking is: would a great coach want to know this before they started working with me? If yes, it belongs in the file.

What to Include

Injuries and physical history. Any significant injury — one that required recovery time, that changed how you train, that you're still managing. Not a medical report. Just: what happened, when, and whether it still affects you.

Formative moments. The experiences that changed how you see yourself as a competitor. Some are positive — the coach who told you something true and good about yourself, the game where you realized what you were capable of. Some are harder — being cut, being benched, the season where it all fell apart.

Both kinds shape you. Both are worth naming.

What a new coach wouldn't know. The most open-ended field — and often the most useful. If you were starting with a new coach tomorrow, what context would help them coach you well? Maybe it's the season you almost quit. Maybe it's the thing your last coach got wrong about you. Write whatever comes to mind.

What This Looks Like in Your File

## Background and Context

Key history: Tore my ACL junior year of high school. 14-month recovery.
I came back faster but I changed — I play safer now than I used to and I'm not sure that's right.

Formative moment: My club coach told me I was a competitor, not just an athlete. I'd never heard it put that way. I still think about it. What a new coach wouldn't know: I went through a period where I almost quit.

I stayed. That decision matters to me.

Part Two: Current State

Why This Section Is Different

Every other section in your file is relatively stable. Your identity doesn't change week to week. Your performance profile, your mental patterns, your history, your coaching style — these are the permanent architecture of who you are as a competitor.

Current State is different. It's not a description of who you are — it's a description of where you are. Right now. Today. This week.

And it's the section that makes everything else come alive.

Here's why it matters. You could build a perfect athlete.md — rich, honest, specific — and then use it unchanged for six months. The Identity section will still be accurate. The Performance Profile will still be accurate.

But the file won't know that your shoulder has been tight for two weeks. It won't know that your coach just changed your role. It won't know that you had the best practice of your season yesterday. It won't know you barely slept or that something is off at home.

Without that, AI gives you good advice for the stable version of you — not the version of you that exists right now.

Current State closes that gap.

When to Update It

You don't need to update Current State every day. You're not keeping a diary.

Update it before it matters:
- The night before a competition
- After a hard loss or a significant setback
- When something changes — your role, your body, your team dynamic
- When you're about to use AI to work through something real
- When you notice something is off and you can't quite name it

Five minutes. A few sentences. That's all it takes to shift AI from responding to your baseline to responding to where you actually are.

Date it every time — it's a signal that this section is meant to change.

The Four Fields

Physically. Where is your body right now? Not your overall health baseline — the current report. Tired or fresh. Heavy or light. Anything that's been nagging. You don't need medical language. "Shoulders are heavy after two weeks of hard training" is enough.

Mentally. What's your headspace? Good anxious or bad anxious. Locked in or scattered. Carrying something or clear. If you're not sure, try: on a scale of locked in to checked out, where are you? And what's pushing you in that direction?

What's on your mind. Separate from mental state — this is the specific thing taking up space. Sometimes it's about performance. Sometimes it's not about performance at all — something at home, stress from school, something that has nothing to do with your sport but lives in your body when you compete. You don't have to explain it in detail. Just name it.

What you need. The most direct field — and the one athletes most often skip. What do you actually need from this conversation with AI? Not reassurance. Not a plan. What do you need? Tell AI and it will go there, instead of guessing and landing somewhere that doesn't help.

What This Looks Like in Your File

## Current State (Updated: March 2)
Physically: Tired. Two weeks of hard training. Shoulders are heavy.
Mentally: Anxious about conference. Good anxious, I think — but I'm watching it.
What's on my mind: Coach moved me to the 400 IM. I didn't ask for it.
Still figuring out how I feel.

That entry takes a general AI and turns it into something that knows exactly where you are.

Chapter 10

What AI Can and Can't Do

This is the honesty chapter.

Everything before this has been about what AI can do when you give it context. This chapter is about where that ends — and why understanding the limit makes everything before it more trustworthy, not less.

The Line

AI is a thinking partner. It is not a decision-maker. It is not a therapist. It is not a coach who has watched you play. It is not your teammate, your parent, or the person who loves you and has been in your corner for years.

Those things matter in ways AI cannot replicate. And trying to use AI as a replacement for them is the most common mistake athletes make once they discover how useful it can be.

Here's where the line actually sits.

What AI Does Well

Thinking things through. This is where AI is genuinely excellent. You have a situation — a coaching decision you're processing, a performance you're trying to understand, a goal you're trying to clarify. AI can help you think it through systematically, without ego, without an agenda, without getting tired. Available at 11pm before a competition the same way it is at noon on a Tuesday.

Reflecting patterns back. When AI knows your file, it can notice things you can't see because you're inside them. The pattern that connects this week to three months ago. The gap between what you said and what you meant. The habit that shows up in how you describe every hard moment. AI doesn't know you the way a human does — but it holds the text of your file consistently and can draw connections across it.

Holding you accountable to what you said. AI has no investment in making you feel better. It will ask the hard question. It will remind you of what you said your process goal was when you're describing a week where you skipped it. It will notice when you're describing a pattern you told it you wanted to change. That consistency — without agenda, without a bad day getting in the way — is genuinely useful.

Being available. Most athletes don't have access to a mental performance coach. The one who would sit with you at 10pm before the hardest competition of your season and just think it through with you. AI isn't that person. But it's available, it's consistent, and it knows your file. For the thinking-out-loud work, that's often enough.

What AI Cannot Do

Make the decision. AI can help you see a decision more clearly. It can surface considerations you haven't named, reflect the tradeoffs back, ask what you'd advise a friend in your situation. But the decision is yours. Athletes who outsource decisions to AI are using it wrong — and usually know it, even if they're not ready to say so.

Be present with you in the moment. The decision at the line of scrimmage, the moment before the race, the choice to push in the final lap — those happen in your body, in real time, with everything you've trained bearing down on a single instant. AI cannot be there. What AI can do is help you prepare for that moment. But the moment itself belongs to you.

Replace human support. There is something that happens between two people who trust each other — an athlete and a coach, an athlete and a teammate, an athlete and a parent — that has no AI equivalent. The felt sense of being known and believed in. The presence of someone who has watched you struggle and stayed anyway. That relationship has a texture that AI doesn't have. If you're using AI to avoid needing those relationships, something is wrong — not with AI, but with how you're using it.

Know what it doesn't know. AI responds to what's in the file. If something important isn't there — something you haven't named, something you don't have language for yet — AI won't know to ask about it. This is why the file matters, and why keeping it honest and current matters. A file with gaps produces help with gaps.

Tell you your values. This is the most important one. AI can reflect your stated values back to you. It can notice when your behavior seems inconsistent with something you said you believed. But it cannot tell you what matters. It cannot tell you what kind of competitor you want to be, or what you're willing to sacrifice, or what it would mean to you to reach the goal versus fall short of it. Those questions are yours — and answering them is some of the most important work you'll do as an athlete. AI can hold the answers once you have them. It cannot produce them.

The Question Worth Asking

After any AI conversation, there's a simple check: Am I using this to think more clearly, or to avoid thinking?

AI can be a way to think more clearly — to surface something you needed to see, to work through something you'd been circling around, to hear your own reasoning reflected back more precisely than you could have expressed it yourself.

AI can also be a way to avoid thinking — to get reassurance without sitting with the discomfort, to hear a plan that doesn't require you to actually decide anything, to stay in the conversation instead of going and doing the work.

The first is useful. The second feels useful but isn't.

You know which one you're doing when you're doing it.

The Bigger Picture

You are an athlete building a relationship with AI at the exact moment that AI is becoming genuinely capable of helping athletes. That's not a small thing.

The athletes who figure this out first — who build real context files, who use AI to think more clearly rather than to avoid thinking, who understand what it can do and what it can't — will have an edge that is genuinely difficult to describe to someone who hasn't experienced it.

Not because AI is magic. Because knowing yourself well enough to explain yourself to something that asks good questions turns out to be one of the most valuable skills an athlete can develop. And building that skill, it turns out, is what this whole book has been about.

You've done the work. The file is yours. Now use it.

The AI you started with didn't know you. The one you use now does. That's not a small thing.
