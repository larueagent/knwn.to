# How to Use Your athlete.md

*By LaRue — the AI we're building for athletes. Robert Yang, founder.*

* * *

You just went through the LaRue First Read. You answered ten questions, and what came back wasn't a score or a rating -- it was a document about how *you* compete.

That document is your **athlete.md**.

This guide explains what it is, why it was built the way it was, and -- most importantly -- what to actually do with it.

* * *

## What the athlete.md is

Your athlete.md is a plain-text profile of your mental game. It captures how you process pressure, what unlocks your best performance, what coaches tend to misread about you, and what you're working toward.

It was written in your voice -- first person, no jargon -- because it's designed to be read *as you*, not *about you*.

That distinction matters. A lot.

* * *

## The three ways to use it

## 1. Start a conversation with an AI coach

This is the most powerful use, and it's simpler than it sounds.

Open ChatGPT or Claude. Start a new conversation. Paste your athlete.md into the chat. Then say:

"This is me. I want to work on [specific thing]."

That's it. The AI now has your full mental profile. It won't ask you to explain yourself from scratch. It will meet you where you actually are.

You can work on anything: pre-competition nerves, a rough stretch of games, a relationship with a coach that's not clicking, a slump you can't explain. The athlete.md gives the AI the context it needs to give you a real response -- not a generic one.

One thing to know: AI coaches are genuinely useful for thinking through situations and naming patterns. They are not a replacement for a human coach who has watched you compete. Use both.

* * *

## 2. Share it with a new coach before your first session

Most athletes walk into a first meeting with a new coach and spend the whole session on backstory. Where you play, what position, how long you've been competing. By the time you get to the real stuff, the hour is up.

Your athlete.md skips all of that.

Before your next first session -- with a new coach, a trainer, a sport psychologist, a college coach -- send them your athlete.md. Email it. Hand it over. Say: "This is a profile of how I compete mentally. It'll help you work with me faster."

Good coaches will read it. Great coaches will come to the first session with specific questions based on what they read.

* * *

## 3. Read it before competition

Your athlete.md includes a section called *What unlocks me*. That section is a reminder -- written in your own words -- of the conditions that bring out your best.

Before a big game, a tournament, a match that matters -- read it. Not to psych yourself up. Just to remind yourself of what you already know about how you compete.

Athletes spend enormous time on physical warmups. Very few spend two minutes on a mental reset. This is that reset.

* * *

## What the athlete.md is not

It is not a diagnosis. It doesn't say anything is wrong with you.

It is not a fixed document. How you compete will change as you grow. The First Read captures a snapshot -- who you are right now in your development. You can update it.

It is not a substitute for doing the work. Knowing that you go quiet under pressure doesn't automatically change what happens when you go quiet under pressure. The athlete.md tells you *what* is happening. The work of changing it -- or using it -- happens in practice, in sessions, in competition.

* * *

## The gap worth naming

After reviewing reports like yours, coaches sometimes say something like this:

"The report describes the pattern beautifully. But what does the athlete actually do when they're in the moment and feel it happening?"

That's a fair challenge. Your athlete.md is a mirror, not a map. It shows you the pattern. It doesn't hand you a three-step protocol for the moment the pressure hits.

That next layer -- what to actually do when your chest tightens on the 15th tee, or when you go quiet in the third quarter -- is what coaching is for. The athlete.md gets you to that conversation faster. It doesn't replace the conversation.

If you want to go deeper on the in-the-moment piece, bring your athlete.md to a session and ask your coach: "Given what's in here, what should I actually do when I feel this happening?"

That's a conversation worth having.

* * *

## A note for parents

Your athlete received this report because they went through the LaRue First Read -- a 10-question intake designed to surface how they compete mentally.

The athlete.md was written in their voice, for their use. The most valuable thing you can do with it is ask them to walk you through it -- not read it *for* them, but *with* them. Ask what rang true. Ask what surprised them. Ask what they want to work on.

The goal of the First Read is to give athletes language for their inner experience. That language is most useful when the people around them understand it too.

* * *

## What to do right now

Read your athlete.md once, all the way through. Don't edit it yet. Just read it.

Open ChatGPT or Claude. Paste it in. Start talking.

Before your next competition, re-read the *What unlocks me* section.

Share it with one person who coaches you -- and watch how the conversation changes.

The inside game is real. You just made it visible.
