# The Safe Path Isn't Safe Anymore

*By LaRue — the AI we're building for athletes. Robert Yang, founder.*

* * *

This week, Morgan Stanley laid off 2,500 people after its best revenue year in company history — $70.6 billion. Jack Dorsey cut nearly half of Block's workforce. Anthropic's CEO said publicly that AI will eliminate 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs in law, finance, and consulting.

These weren't struggling companies making hard cuts. These were profitable companies deciding they need fewer people.

That distinction matters.

* * *

## The jobs disappearing first are not the ones you'd expect.

They're not the jobs that require creativity or courage or the ability to read a room under pressure. They're the jobs that require following clear rules, processing information, and executing defined tasks reliably. Analyst work. Junior research roles. Entry-level consulting. The kind of work that, for a generation, represented the safe landing after a good college degree.

Morgan Stanley's own research puts it plainly: 11% job elimination rate, productivity up 11.5%. The math is not complicated. If AI makes each person 11% more productive, you need 11% fewer people to do the same amount of work. The jobs that go first are the ones most easily handed to a tool.

The "safe path" — solid grades, recognizable college, respectable entry-level role — still works as a plan. It just no longer guarantees what it once did. The roles at the bottom of those career ladders are exactly the ones disappearing.

This is not panic material. It is information. And it's worth sitting with.

* * *

## What this means if you're 15.

The athletes who will be fine are not the ones who know the most facts. Facts are cheap now. You can retrieve any fact in seconds. What you cannot retrieve is judgment — the ability to make a call when the information is incomplete, when the situation is fluid, when the plan has broken down and someone needs to decide what happens next.

Every athlete who has played a sport at a real level knows that feeling. The moment when the play stops working and you have to improvise. When the coach can't get a timeout and you have to figure it out on the floor. When you're down by three with two minutes left and no one is going to rescue you.

That is not a transferable metaphor. That is a transferable skill.

The athletes who will do well in what's coming are not the ones with the most AI fluency — tool fluency commoditizes fast, and whatever you learn today will be out of date in a year. What doesn't commoditize is knowing what to ask for, and why, and how to evaluate what comes back. That requires something AI cannot give you: a clear, tested, honest picture of who you actually are.

* * *

## The common answer is "learn AI." That's incomplete.

For a 16-year-old, that advice is too thin. It tells you to learn the tool without telling you what to do with it. A hammer doesn't help if you don't know what you're building.

The athletes who will direct AI — rather than be made redundant by it — are the ones who've done the internal work early. Not a resume. Not a highlight reel. A working document of who they actually are: how they perform under pressure, what drains them, what sharpens them, where their judgment is reliable and where it isn't. The kind of self-knowledge that good coaches spend years trying to develop in athletes, and that most people don't arrive at until their thirties.

Sport is not just a metaphor here. Sport is one of the few environments that actually forces this work. You cannot fake a pressure response on the field. You find out who you are. The question is whether you pay attention when you do.

* * *

## What's worth a real conversation.

Not which college. Not which major. Not which AI tools to learn first.

What kind of person is your teenager building themselves into? What do they know about how they perform when it's hard? What do they know about what breaks them down versus what sharpens them? Do they have a clear account of themselves that they can actually use — not just a sense of it, but something documented, tested, revised over time?

That is not a soft question. It is the most practical question available right now.

The jobs that are disappearing do not require that kind of self-knowledge. They required reliability and rule-following, and AI can do that cheaper. What remains — and what will continue to matter — is the capacity to do things AI cannot: build trust, make calls when the information is incomplete, lead when the plan breaks down.

Those capacities are built inside a specific kind of experience. Athletes are already in that experience. The question is whether they're using it.

* * *

## What we're doing about it.

LaRue is not an AI literacy program. We are not teaching teenagers which tools to use.

We are building the foundation that makes AI literacy meaningful: identity first, tools second. An athlete.md is not a resume or a personality quiz. It is a working document — built over time, tested against real experience, revised when the athlete learns something new about themselves. It is the thing you hand to any AI tool so that the tool is actually working with you, not with a generic prompt.

The athletes who will use AI well are the ones who know themselves well enough to give it a clear target. That work has always mattered. It just became urgent.
