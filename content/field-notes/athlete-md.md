# athlete.md

*A document for what you already know about yourself*

You've been competing for years. You know your forty time. You know your shooting percentage. You know your combine numbers, your rankings, your highlight reel.

None of that tells you why you go quiet in the fourth quarter.

None of it tells you why you play your best when no one's watching, and lock up the moment the scout arrives. Why feedback from a certain coach lands like a gut punch and stays there. Why you can flip a switch mid-game when the emotion is right – and can't find that switch when it isn't.

That knowledge lives somewhere. It's already in you. You've just never had a document for it.

* * *

The athlete.md is that document.

Not a scouting report. Not a psych eval. Not a coach's impression of you or a parent's version of who you are.

Yours.

It's written in first person, in plain language, without the vocabulary of performance science or sports psychology. It doesn't rate you. It doesn't tell you what's wrong with you or what to fix. It tells you – specifically, precisely – how you actually compete.

How you get into your best state. What derails you. What coaches consistently misread. What you need from the people around you that you've probably never said out loud. Where you are right now in your development, and what the next real step looks like.

A 15-year-old should be able to read it and think: *that's me.*

An AI system should be able to load it as context and understand who you are before you type a word.

Both of those things are true at once. That's the point.

* * *

Here's what's strange about the athlete.md:

You didn't write it. But it's written as you.

You answered ten questions – honestly, offhandedly, in your own language. LaRue read those answers and produced a structured map of your competitive psychology. Then it rendered that map as a document in your voice. First person. Present tense. Specific enough to be useful.

Not a summary of what you said. Not a paraphrase. A document that names what you already know about yourself but have never had the words for.

The patterns in it – how you carry mistakes, what unlocks you, how you read the room during a game – aren't things someone decided about you. They emerged. From years of competing. From what worked and what didn't. From every coach who got you right and every one who got you wrong.

LaRue just made them legible. Not to tell you what's broken. To show you how you actually work – so you can move.

* * *

Why does this matter?

You've spent thousands of hours getting your body ready. Film. Weights. Conditioning. Drills. All of it visible, measurable, improvable.

Your mental game? It stays invisible. You can't put it on video. So the patterns that actually drive your performance – how you handle mistakes, what unlocks you, why you play different in games than practice – never get worked on. They just repeat.

The athlete.md makes that invisible stuff visible. Not so a coach can analyze you. So you can work with it. Load it before a game. Share it with a new coach so they don't spend three months misreading you. Keep it. Own it.

Here's what changes when you have it: You stop blaming yourself for things you didn't understand. You start adjusting because you know what to adjust. You know why you went quiet in the fourth quarter, and you know what shifts that. That's agency. That's the difference between hoping you'll be different next time and knowing exactly what needs to happen.

* * *

What the athlete.md is not:

It is not a diagnosis. There is nothing in it that says something is wrong with you.

It is not permanent. How you compete changes as you grow. The First Read captures a snapshot – who you are right now, in this season of your development.

It is not the work. Knowing that you go quiet under pressure does not automatically change what happens when you go quiet under pressure. The document tells you what is happening. What you do with that is the actual work.

* * *

You've always known things about yourself that were hard to say.

That you need to feel trusted before you can play free. That you process alone before you can talk. That you're sharper when the moment is real than when you're being watched and graded.

The athlete.md says it for you.
