_By LaRue — the AI we're building for athletes. Robert Yang, founder._

* * *

A product manager named Peter Yang published a guide this week on how to become "AI native" in five levels. It's good. It's also written entirely for adults building software products.

I want to give you the athlete version.

The five levels are real. The progression is real. But the examples — fixing toilets with ChatGPT, building apps, optimizing meeting notes — don't map to what a 15-year-old athlete actually needs. So here's the same framework, translated.

Most athletes are stuck at Level 1. That's not a criticism. That's where almost everyone is. But the gap between Level 1 and Level 3 is enormous, and you can close it faster than you think.

* * *

## Level 1: AI for Everyday Answers

_Where 99% of athletes are today._

You're using ChatGPT or Gemini like a better Google. You ask it questions. It answers. You move on.

This looks like:
- "What should I eat before a game?"
- "Why does my hip flexor keep getting tight?"
- "What GPA do I need to play D1 soccer?"

This is not nothing. Getting a real answer in thirty seconds instead of scrolling five websites is genuinely useful. But it's also the minimum. You're treating AI like a search engine with better sentences.

The limitation: the AI knows nothing about you. It is answering for a generic athlete — one who plays your sport, roughly, in some general context. That athlete is not you. And the gap between a generic answer and an answer built around who you actually are is not small.

If this is all you're doing, you're barely at the surface. You need to get to Level 2.

* * *

## Level 2: AI for Daily Training Life

_A significant step up. Most athletes don't get here. You should._

Level 2 is where AI stops being a search engine and starts being a tool that works with your actual life.

This looks like:
- **Voice journaling after practice.** You don't type. You talk. "I felt flat tonight. Coach pulled me aside about my footwork. I think I was tired from the game Saturday." Three minutes. AI transcribes and organizes it. Over time, that log becomes a real record of your season — patterns you'd never catch just living inside it.

- **Prepping for hard conversations.** A conversation with your coach about playing time. A call with a college recruiter. A difficult talk with your parents about whether to keep going. You brief AI on the situation and your history, ask it to help you prepare, ask it what you might be missing. The conversation goes better.

- **Reviewing your own performance.** Not film — your own written account of what happened and why. What felt off. What clicked. What you want to do differently. AI helps you ask better questions of yourself.

**Probably 80% of the value athletes will ever get from AI lives at this level.** The athletes who build Level 2 habits in the next year will have a real advantage over those who don't — not because AI is magic, but because consistent documentation and reflection compound. A coach who has watched you for two years knows things about you that a coach who just arrived does not. That's what Level 2 is building: a record that gets smarter the longer you keep it.

You don't need any technical skill to get here. You need consistency.

* * *

## Level 3: AI for Building Your Portrait

_This is where it gets different._

In Peter Yang's version, Level 3 is about prototyping software. Building a rough version of an app before you spec it out.

For athletes, Level 3 is about prototyping yourself.

Most athletes have a vague sense of who they are as competitors. They know they're "competitive" or "coachable" or "a team player." Those words are almost useless. They're the athlete version of a resume that says "hardworking and detail-oriented."

Level 3 is the work of getting specific.

This looks like:
- Sitting down with AI and answering the questions you've never been asked in a structured way. Not "how do you perform under pressure?" but: "Walk me through the last time you performed really well when the stakes were high. What was true about your preparation? What was your mindset in the warmup? What do you remember about the moment it clicked?" Then: "Now walk me through the last time you underperformed in a big moment. What was different?"
- Building an athlete.md — a document that holds your real portrait. Not a resume. Not a bio. A working file that captures how you perform, what drains you, what sharpens you, how you respond to different coaching styles, what your sport has taught you about yourself.
- Handing that file to any AI tool and immediately getting a response that feels like it actually knows you. Not the generic athlete — you.

This is hard work. Not technically hard — the tools are simple. Personally hard. Most people, adults included, have never answered these questions seriously. Athletes who do it at 15 or 16 have something that most 30-year-olds don't: a clear, tested, honest account of themselves.

That is not a soft skill. That is infrastructure. It makes everything else — AI tools, recruiting conversations, coaching relationships, eventually interviews and careers — work better.

* * *

## Level 4: AI Knows You Before You Ask

_You're not using AI anymore. AI is working for you._

Level 4 is where your portrait stops being a document you consult and starts being a context layer that travels with you.

This looks like:
- A morning check-in that reads your state — sleep, soreness, stress, what happened yesterday — and tells you what kind of practice day to expect and why. Not generic advice. Advice calibrated to your history and your current moment.
- An agent that preps your coach for your session because it has read your athlete.md, your last three practice logs, and your current state, and has summarized what you need today.
- AI that flags when your self-reported patterns don't match what it knows about you. "You said you felt fine, but the last three times you wrote this way before a game, you underperformed. Here's what was different on the days you were right."

At Level 4, AI is not a tool you open. It is a system that runs underneath your athletic life, reading your context continuously, surfacing things you would otherwise miss.

You don't build Level 4 by learning more tools. You build it by doing Levels 2 and 3 consistently until your context layer is deep enough to actually be useful. Level 4 is not a skill. It is an outcome of doing the earlier work seriously.

* * *

## Level 5: Your Personal Agent

_This is LaRue._

Level 5 is when everything comes together: a personal AI agent that knows your full portrait, tracks your current state, reads your goals, and works on your behalf — not just when you ask it something, but continuously.

The athlete at Level 5 doesn't ask "what should I focus on in practice today?" Their agent already knows. It read their athlete.md. It checked their state.md. It saw the game film from Tuesday. It knows they're working on a technical flaw under pressure and that this week's schedule gives them two sessions to address it before the next competition. The conversation starts there, not from scratch.

Level 5 is not far away. The infrastructure exists. The gap is at Levels 2 and 3 — most athletes haven't built the portrait that makes Level 5 meaningful. A powerful agent with no context is just a fast search engine. A powerful agent with a deep portrait is something else entirely.

This is what we're building toward at LaRue. Not the tool — the foundation that makes the tool worth having.

## Where You Actually Are

Here's the honest version of this framework for most athletes right now:

**Level 1:** You've used ChatGPT. You ask it questions.

**Level 2:** You've thought about using AI more systematically but haven't built the habit yet.

**Level 3:** You've never built a document of who you actually are as a competitor. You know things about yourself, but they live in your head, unstructured.

**Level 4:** Not there yet — and that's fine. You can't skip to it.

**Level 5:** You're reading this, which means you're thinking about it. That puts you ahead of most.

The path from Level 1 to Level 3 doesn't require any technical skill. It requires honest self-reflection and fifteen minutes a day. Most athletes won't do it. The ones who do will find, six months from now, that AI is doing something completely different for them than it is for everyone else.

Not because they learned more tools. Because they gave the tools something real to work with.

* * *

## Start Here

If you're at Level 1 and want to move:

Pick one practice this week. After it ends, open a voice app — Notes, Whisper, anything — and talk for three minutes. What happened? What felt off? What clicked? What does your body feel like right now?

Don't analyze it. Just say it. Let AI transcribe it. Save it.

Do that five times. Then read all five. You will learn something about yourself that you did not know before.

That's Level 2. That's where the real work starts.
