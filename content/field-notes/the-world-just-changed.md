# The World Just Changed. Your Kid Is Already In It.

*By LaRue — the AI we're building for athletes. Robert Yang, founder.*

---

Your kid is already using AI. Every day — homework, training questions, film review, pre-game prep. You know this. That's not the problem.

The problem is the AI they're using doesn't know your kid at all.

---

## Something shifted in February.

Most people missed it.

For the last two years, AI meant chatbots. You type a question, it answers. A better search engine. A faster Google. A lot of parents still think that's what we're talking about.

It isn't anymore.

In February 2026, AI stopped responding and started *doing.* Not waiting to be asked — acting. Booking appointments. Sending emails. Managing tasks. Remembering what happened last week and building on it. The technology world has a name for this: agents. You don't need the word. What you need is the implication.

The kids coming into the workforce in three years aren't competing for jobs in the AI world most adults imagined. They're competing in one that moved faster than almost anyone expected.

---

## Your kid knows something is happening.

They're not oblivious. They're worried.

83% of Gen Z uses AI tools daily. And 1 in 5 of them is "very concerned" that AI will eliminate their job within two years. 64% believe entry-level work is going to be harder to find — not easier.

They're using the technology and they're scared of it at the same time. That's not a contradiction. That's what it feels like to be inside a shift you can't fully see.

The anxiety is real. For a lot of young athletes, it's showing up in ways that look like something else — distraction, pressure, that low-grade sense that nothing they're doing is quite enough.

---

## You're not the only parent who feels this.

55% of parents are anxious about whether their child is being prepared for an AI-driven economy. The most common words they use to describe how they feel: uncertain. Worried. Concerned. Cautious.

Two-thirds are questioning what college is even worth now. Not in a cynical way. In a genuinely uncertain way. The script that worked for your generation — study hard, get the degree, climb the ladder — doesn't feel like it maps to what's coming.

That's not panic. That's accurate. The script did change. The question is what replaces it.

---

## The gap that actually matters — especially for athletes.

Here's what most of the conversation misses.

AI isn't the threat. Generic AI is the threat. The version that knows nothing about your specific kid — their mental game, their pressure patterns, what breaks them down and what builds them up — and gives them the same response it would give anyone.

That kind of AI makes the averages smarter. It doesn't develop anyone.

An athlete who has AI that actually *knows them* — that has their history, understands how they respond to adversity, can tell the difference between a bad day and a real pattern — isn't just ahead on some tech curve. They have something most of their peers don't have.

Self-knowledge. And a system that works with it instead of ignoring it.

That's not a small edge. In sport, and in everything that comes after sport, knowing yourself clearly — knowing how you perform under pressure, what drains you, what sharpens you — is the foundation everything else is built on.

The athletes who figure that out early aren't just better athletes. They're better at being themselves. Which turns out to be the skill that transfers everywhere.

---

## What we're building.

We're early. We want to be honest about that.

LaRue is an AI built specifically for athletes — not a chatbot, not a generic wellness app. The idea is simple: an AI that starts by learning who your athlete actually is, builds on that over time, and gets more useful the more they use it. Not because the AI is magic. Because the athlete knows themselves better, and the AI knows the athlete.

We're still learning what that looks like in practice. What it means to build something that actually serves a 16-year-old athlete across a full season. What works, what doesn't, what we got wrong in the first version. That's where we are.

What we know is that this is the right problem to work on. The athletes who develop real self-knowledge early — who understand their patterns, their pressure responses, what sharpens them and what drains them — don't just perform better. They carry that into everything that comes after sport.

We're building toward that. If you want to follow along — or if you have an athlete who wants to be part of figuring this out with us — we'd love to hear from you.
